What Is the SSDI Gross-Up Rule?

Since SSDI is often tax-free, lenders can increase your qualifying income to reflect your actual purchasing power. This is called "grossing up."

FHA: $2,840 SSDI × 1.15 = $3,266 qualifying income
Conventional: $2,840 SSDI × 1.25 = $3,550 qualifying income

This difference can mean $30,000-$60,000 more in buying power. The gross-up only applies to the tax-free portion of SSDI.

Required Documents for SSDI Mortgage

  • SSA Award Letter (showing monthly benefit amount)
  • 12 months bank statements showing SSDI deposits
  • Proof of continuance (most SSA award letters serve this purpose)
  • If a review is pending: additional documentation may be required
  • Standard mortgage docs: ID, credit report, bank statements

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I get a mortgage on SSDI alone with no other income?
Yes, if your SSDI after gross-up is sufficient for the DTI. On $2,840/month SSDI (grossed to $3,550 conventional), your maximum home at 36% DTI with no other debts is approximately $280,000 with 20% down. FHA allows higher DTI, potentially qualifying you for more.
Does SSDI count as income for all mortgage types?
Yes. FHA, conventional (Fannie Mae/Freddie Mac), VA, and USDA all accept SSDI as qualifying income. The gross-up rate and maximum DTI vary by program. VA loans are most flexible for veterans receiving VA disability payments, which may also be exempt from the funding fee.
What if my SSDI is under review or I have a scheduled continuing disability review?
A scheduled CDR (Continuing Disability Review) can complicate qualification. Lenders need reasonable assurance the income will continue for 3+ years. If a CDR is imminent, some lenders may decline or require additional documentation. Consult with an FHA-approved lender experienced with disability income.
Can I combine SSDI with other income?
Absolutely. SSDI + W2 income, SSDI + pension, SSDI + part-time work — all can be combined. Each income type is qualified separately then added together. This is one of the most common SSDI borrower profiles and lenders handle it routinely.
