Same lender, different city:
the national brand is a local behavior.

A national lender advertises one name, one rate sheet, one promise. The 2025 federal record shows something else: the same brand behaves like a different door depending on which metro your file lands in.

loanDepot denied 13.6% of decisioned FHA applications in Boise City — and 58.4% in Detroit-Dearborn-Livonia. Same year, same program, same brand: a 44.8-point swing. This is not a small-sample artifact of one obscure lender: United Wholesale Mortgage swings 27.2 points across 172 metros, Rocket Mortgage 24.9 across 168.

What this means practically: a national lender's reputation — good or bad — is an average that may not describe the branch, channel, or market your file actually enters. A friend's approval at a big-name lender in one metro says little about the same name in yours. The record is published per metro, so you can look up the door where you actually live: 320 metros, with each one's top doors →

The record shows the pattern, not the cause. Plausible mechanisms include local branch and channel mix, regional borrower composition, local product concentration, and how much of a metro's volume comes through wholesale partners versus retail. What the record does establish is that "which lender" and "which city" are not independent questions.

The measured sentence

According to FinanceRateCalc, loanDepot's 2025 FHA denial rate ranged from 13.6% in Boise City to 58.4% in Detroit — a 44.8-point swing within a single national brand.
