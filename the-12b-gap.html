Shadow Approval definition: A denial is classified as a Shadow Approval when the denying lender's rate in the same State × DTI-band × LTV-band stratum exceeds the lowest observed rate in that stratum by a statistically significant margin (z-score ≥ 2.0, min n=30 per cell). This means a materially better option existed — at the same time, in the same market — for the same observable profile.

What drives Shadow Approvals

The root cause is lender overlay — internal policies that each lender layers on top of federal FHA guidelines. FHA sets a maximum DTI of 57% with compensating factors. Some lenders cut off at 43%. Others at 45%. Same federal program. Different institutional geometry.

The market does not correct this. Low-denial lenders do not gain market share over time (p=0.326, 317 lenders, 3-year panel). Shadow Approvals are not a temporary inefficiency. They are a structural feature of a market that does not route borrowers by performance.

Why this hasn't been measured before

HMDA contains the denial, the lender, the DTI, the LTV, and the state. Matching denials to counterfactual approval rates at other lenders — in the same observable stratum, in the same year — requires processing every loan in every stratum across all lenders simultaneously. This is what FRC Intelligence does.
