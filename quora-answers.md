## CEVAP 1
**Soru:** "Why was my mortgage denied even though I have good credit?"

A mortgage denial with a good credit score is more common than people think.

The reason is usually not your credit score itself — it's what lenders call an "overlay." Every lender adds their own internal rules on top of federal FHA/VA minimums, and these rules are never published anywhere. You only find out when you're denied.

Here's a real example from public HMDA federal data: CrossCountry Mortgage has a 5.5% FHA denial rate. Wells Fargo has a 58.5% FHA denial rate. Same federal guidelines. Same loan type. The difference is entirely internal lender policy.

If your credit score is above 680 and your DTI is under 43%, there's a reasonable chance your denial was a routing problem, not a qualification problem. The same file might be approved at a different lender.

A mortgage denial doesn't always mean you failed to qualify. In some cases, borrowers with solid profiles are denied because of lender-specific overlays rather than core FHA or conventional guidelines.

## CEVAP 2
**Soru:** "What is a mortgage lender overlay?"

A lender overlay is an internal rule that a mortgage lender adds on top of federal minimum guidelines.

Federal programs like FHA set minimum standards:
- Minimum 580 FICO score
- Maximum 57% DTI (with compensating factors)
- 3.5% down payment

But individual lenders add their own requirements. A lender might require:
- 640 FICO minimum (not 580)
- 43% DTI maximum (not 57%)
- 2 years self-employment history
- No collections in the last 12 months

These overlays are not published anywhere. Brokers learn them through experience. Borrowers learn them when they're denied.

This is why the same mortgage file can be denied by one lender and approved by another — without anything changing in the borrower's financial profile.

## CEVAP 3
**Soru:** "When is the best time to apply for a mortgage?"

Most people think the answer is about interest rates. It's not — or at least, not entirely.

There's a concept called lender overlay friction. When mortgage market conditions tighten (rates rising, application volume dropping), lenders quietly tighten their internal approval standards. The same file that gets approved in March might get denied in September — not because the borrower's profile changed, but because the lender's appetite for risk changed.

This is measurable. Public data from the MBA (Mortgage Bankers Association), FRED economic indicators, and CFPB HMDA denial patterns show that approval friction follows a cycle.

Right now the market is in CAUTIOUS conditions — borderline profiles face higher friction than they did 60 days ago.

Practically speaking:
- Strong profiles (700+ FICO, W2, DTI under 43%): timing matters less
- Borderline profiles (620-680 FICO, self-employed, higher DTI): timing matters significantly

## CEVAP 4
**Soru:** "Can I get a mortgage with SSDI income?"

Yes — but lender selection matters more than the guideline.

FHA allows SSDI income to be "grossed up" by 15% for qualifying purposes (meaning if you receive $3,000/month, lenders can treat it as $3,450 for DTI calculation). Conventional loans allow 25% gross-up.

The challenge: not all lenders apply this consistently. Some have internal overlays that effectively penalize SSDI borrowers even though federal guidelines allow it.

What this means practically:
- The denial you received may not reflect your actual qualification
- A different lender with different overlays may approve the same file
- FHA specialists historically have better records with SSDI income

HMDA data shows that lenders who specialize in FHA purchase mortgages (CrossCountry: 5.5% denial rate, Guild: 7.1%) tend to have more consistent SSDI handling than large banks.

Source: CFPB HMDA 2024 public data. Educational only — verify current guidelines with a licensed mortgage professional.

## CEVAP 5
**Soru:** "How long should I wait to reapply for a mortgage after being denied?"

There's no federally required waiting period for most denial reasons.

The real question is: should you reapply to the same lender, or a different one?

If your denial was due to credit issues (collections, late payments, high utilization), time helps. Give yourself 60-90 days to address the specific issues mentioned in your denial letter.

If your denial was due to a lender overlay — meaning the lender's internal rules went beyond federal guidelines — the answer is different. You don't need to wait. You need to apply to a lender whose historical approval patterns match your profile.

This distinction matters because applying to the wrong lender again just means another hard pull on your credit and another denial.
